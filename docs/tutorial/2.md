### 🧐 What does `-returns` mean?

Let's break down the expression we just used. First, `returns` is a data field containing each company's return for every date.

| Date       | MSFT    | HOG     | AAPL    | GOOG     | PG      |
|------------|---------|---------|---------|----------|---------|
| 2010-01-05 | 0.032%  | 0.745%  | 0.173%  | -0.441%  | 0.033%  |
| 2010-01-06 | -0.613% | -0.234% | -1.591% | -2.522%  | -0.474% |
| 2010-01-07 | -1.033% | 0.820%  | -0.185% | -2.328%  | -0.542% |
| 2010-01-08 | 0.683%  | -1.047% | 0.665%  | 1.333%   | -0.132% |

Here, we added a minus sign in front of `returns`. This means we're betting *against* returns.

So, the formula you just ran expresses the idea of investing in the opposite direction of yesterday's company returns. In other words:  
- Companies with **high returns yesterday** are expected to have **price decreases**.  
- Companies with **low returns yesterday** are expected to have **price increases**.

As you can see, simply investing opposite to returns can create an Alpha that generates quite decent results. This concept is known as **Price Reversion**. We'll explore this in more detail soon.

---

### 👍 What is an Alpha?

At WorldQuant, an **Alpha** is a mathematical model that seeks to predict the future price movement of financial instruments.

---

### 🔧 Operators

Operators transform data fields. You can think of the minus sign in front of `returns` as a form of operator.
